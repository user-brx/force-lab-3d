🚶 Walking person

Newton's laws (2nd and 3rd) and static friction.

The idea

Nobody pushes themselves forward alone. When you walk, your foot pushes the ground backward; by Newton's 3rd law, the ground pushes you forward with the same force. That force only exists because of friction between foot and ground - on ice the foot slips and there is almost no propulsion.

The force the ground returns is a static friction force: it adapts to what's needed, but has a ceiling. If you ask for more than that ceiling, you slip.

The formulas

Fnet = m · a 2nd law: acceleration is proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to mass.
FA→B = − FB→A 3rd law: action and reaction have equal magnitude, opposite direction, on different bodies.
fstatic ≤ μs · N   |   fkinetic = μk · N N = normal force (= m·g on flat ground). If the required propulsion exceeds μs·N, it slips and μk·N applies.

The simulator also shows the "reaction on the planet": the same force goes to Earth, but since aEarth = F / MEarth and MEarth ≈ 6×10²⁴ kg, the planet's acceleration is negligible (~10⁻²³ m/s²).

Worked example

70 kg person on asphalt. Weight = N = m·g = 70 × 9.81 = 686.5 N.

Static friction ceiling = μs·N = 0.9 × 686.5 = 617.9 N. Since the typical walking muscle force (a few tens of newtons) is far below that, the foot does not slip and all the force becomes propulsion.

On ice the ceiling drops to μs·N = 0.1 × 686.5 = 68.6 N; asking for more makes it slip, and the propulsion is limited to μk·N = 0.03 × 686.5 ≈ 20.6 N.
