🚀 Rocket

Thrust by mass expulsion, the Tsiolkovsky equation, TWR and specific impulse.

The idea

Thrust is born inside the engine: gas is thrown one way and the rocket goes the other with the same force (3rd law / conservation of momentum) - exactly like the rifle. That's why the rocket works (better!) in vacuum: it doesn't push against the air, it pushes the very gas it ejects.

The formulas

Fthrust = ṁ · ve = ṁ · Isp · g₀ ṁ = mass flow (kg/s); ve = exhaust velocity; Isp = specific impulse (s).
Δv = Isp · g₀ · ln( m₀ / mf ) Tsiolkovsky rocket equation: m₀ = initial mass (with fuel), mf = dry mass.
TWR = Fthrust / (m · g) Thrust-to-weight ratio. It only lifts off if TWR > 1. On the Moon (lower g) it lifts with thrust that wouldn't on Earth.
Isp(h) = Isp,vac − (Isp,vac − Isp,sea-level)·(ρ/ρ₀) Thrust grows ~10-20 % in vacuum (no back-pressure from air at the nozzle exit).

Worked example

Mass flow for 30 kN at sea level: ṁ = F/(Isp·g₀) = 30000/(260·9.81) ≈ 11.8 kg/s.

Δv in vacuum with m₀ = 2000 kg and mf = 500 kg: Δv = 311·9.81·ln(2000/500) = 3050·ln(4) = 3050·1.386 ≈ 4228 m/s.

TWR on Earth of a 2000 kg rocket with 30 kN: TWR = 30000/(2000·9.81) ≈ 1.53 → lifts off. On the Moon (g = 1.62) the same thrust gives TWR ≈ 9.3.
